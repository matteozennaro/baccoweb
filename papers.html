Our latest research

Tidal response of structure formation

go to paper

In cosmology we typically make simulation boxes of some finite size and then use periodical boundary conditions to fill in what happens beyond the box. However, this neglects contributions to this boxy patch of the universe from larger scales. The lowest order of these “missing” contributions can be described by a local over-density and an external tidal field.

We have developed “anisotropic separate universe simulations” which can incorporate such tidal fields (and/or overdensities) into the evolution of the background of a simulation. You can imagine that our initially cubic box now gets deformed through the tidal field into a cuboid shape over time. Thereby we simulate a truly anisotropic mini-universe which has different expansion rates in different directions (described by a Hubble tensor instead of a single Hubble parameter)!

These simulations can serve as an excellent testing laboratory for what tidal fields do to the structures in our universe. For example we find that haloes get stretched along the same direction where the tidal field expands the mini-universe the strongest – and squashed in the other directions. However, haloes get stretched/squashed less than the universe overall! Further we measure to high accuracy the response function, which quantifies the impact of the tidal field onto the three dimensional power spectrum. In the future this simulation technique could be used to quantify the intrinsic alignment of galaxies due to tidal fields, and therefore help interpret weak lensing surveys. The scheme is simple and general and could easily be incorporated into state of the art hydrodynamic simulations.

3% accurate scaling over a large parameter space

go to paper

It has been 15 years since the Millennium simulation was complete. It was the result of the collaboration of many people and require to run in a large cluster for 350.000 CPU-hours. Today, running a simulation of similar characteristics can be done by small groups in a small cluster, but still is computationally expensive and require a considerable amount of disk space.

This computational limitation can challenge the run of many different simulations. In particular, running many simulations with different cosmologies is sometimes necessary when studying cosmology from galaxy clustering. To deal with this, Angulo & White 2010 develop a method to 'recycle' dark matter simulations. Their algorithm 'rescale' a dark matter simulation from its original cosmology to a different target cosmology in a matter of seconds.

This technique opens a series of opportunities to study cosmology using N-Body simulation. By running only a small set of simulations, we can scale them to other cosmologies so it can cover a huge cosmological parameter-space. The advantages compare to the traditional method (running tens of simulations in a narrow parameter-space) are that we can focus our computational resources and run larger and higher resolved N-Body simulations instead of many of them, increasing the statistics, the precision and the variety of the mocks we can run over these scaled simulations. In Contreras et al. 2020, we expand the scaling method, test its accuracy over a range of cosmological parameter > 10σ above Planck predictions and present a proof of concept of the constraining power of cosmological parameter using galaxy clustering. We find that this updated version of the scaling algorithm can achieve a 3% precision on the power spectrum of matter, haloes and subhaloes, as is shown in the figure below. We extend this test to z=1 and redshift space finding the same precision.

This is the first of a series of papers that aim to show the constraining of cosmological parameters from the clustering of galaxies of the Universe. In the next papers, we will present an improved way to do mocks with the subhalo abundance matching technique (SHAM), show the accuracy of the scaling technique with the multipoles of the correlation function and the additional constraining power we get from it and test our method on real data. Figure 1: The ratio between the power spectra of scaled and target simulations in real space. The target cosmologies adopt Nenya cosmologies varying one cosmological parameter at a time, as indicated in the label of each figure. The eight columns, from left to right, show changes in σ8, Ωm, Ωb, ns, h, Mν, w0, and wa. The light shaded region highlights a 3% discrepancy. The top row displays results for dark matter, whereas middle and bottom rows do so for haloes with a number density of \(3.16 \times 10^{-3} \, h^3 \, \mathrm{Mpc}^{-3}\) selected according to their mass, and subhaloes with a number density of \(10^{-2} h^3 \, \mathrm{Mpc}^{-3}\) selected according to their peak maximum circular velocity (Vpeak). The dark shaded region shows the region where Poisson shot-noise is above than 80% of the power spectrum amplitude.

Emulating likelihoods

go to paper

Stop assuming, for a second, that you have problems with your theory. Now, let’s say you are super happy with your beloved theoretical model and want to compare with some data. There is some trouble though. Even though you put all of your affection into developing this model, it is still very computationally slow (eg. 4 hours each evaluation for a given set of parameters) and it has some intrinsic noise in its estimations. Do not worry, nobody is perfect. You can still do parameter constraints with it.

In this work, we developed a methodology based on an iterative Gaussian emulation of the target likelihood function to be able to perform posterior parameters constraints with such kind of models. The basic idea is very simple. First, try to have a rough idea of how the posterior distribution looks like (it does not matter if it does not look anything like the real one at this stage), by having some model evaluations on the parameter space.

Second, interpolate via a Gaussian process to the points of parameter space you might be interested in. This interpolation is likely to be very fast unless you already have lots of initial evaluated points (which would be surprising if your model takes 4 hours to compute). Third, find where is the point in parameter space that maximizes your likelihood function from this fancy interpolation and evaluate your terribly slow model there. Finding this point should also be fast since you are not evaluating your theoretical model.

Lastly, redo the Gaussian process interpolation with the new evaluated point. This last point has made your emulation of the likelihood more accurate because you have one more point to do the emulation, right? Then, why not iterating this process until the emulation stops changing because there is no new information in the last suggested points for evaluation? In the paper, we show examples of how this works up to 9 dimensions for a galaxy clustering kind of likelihood.

Spoiler alert, if you decide to do this, you might gain a factor of 100 less needed evaluations compared to an MCMC. Now it does not seem as if your model is too computationally heavy to do science with it, does it?

Adding baryon physics to dark matter simulations

go to paper

Do you need the distribution of dark matter and gas in multiple realizations of the universe, but you lack the million CPU hours and the thousand of cores required to run hundreds of magneto-hydrodynamical simulations? Do you happen to have in your family hard-disk, among the pictures of the holiday in Andalucia and the videos of your cousin's wedding, a N-body simulation? Maybe the techniques described in this paper can make your day.

We show how using a single gravity-only simulation, we are able to reproduce the three-dimensional total mass density field of 7 of the state-of-the-art magneto-hydrodynamical simulations, to better than 1%! To achieve this result, we combine the cosmology scaling with a baryon correction model. In brief, the latter one decompose the density profiles of the halos of your simulation in different components: a central galaxy, gravitationally bound and ejected gas, and of course dark matter (see the Figure). These components are first analitically computed as a function of halo mass and concentration, and afterwards all the single particles are displaced accordingly. The whole process take tipically few seconds in a laptop*!

You can now compute your matter power spectrum, and have a minute to watch again the video of your cousin's wedding without worring about the baryonic effects.

*(the BACCO team does not guarantee a timescale of the method inferior to 1 minute when applied to simulations with trillion particles and gigaparsec boxes.)

Adding neutrinos to \( \Lambda \)CDM simulations

go to paper

Let's say you have a \(\Lambda\)CDM simulation, large size, high mass resolution, but no neutrinos. Should you spend the same computation time (or even more) to rerun it including massive neutrinos? And which neutrino mass should you choose?

With the techinque we presented in this paper, you can apply a transformation of times and lengths to the objects in your simulated cube that enable you to add the effect of an arbitrary mass neutrino component in post procssing. The idea is rooted in the "cosmology rescaling" method that was presented in Angulo & White (2010).

The effect of massive neutrinos, on scales within the neutrino free-streaming length, closely corresponds to looking at a massless neutrino cosmology at an earlier time. This justifies the use of the scaling technique, provided, as we do in this paper, that we correct with an ad-hoc displacement field the large and intermediate scales. The result is pretty nice, as you can see in the figure!

Note that to get this effect not only is it necessary to correct for large scales, but one should also displace in-halo particles to account for the different concentrations halos of the same mass have in different cosmologies. We did it following the same technique explained in Contreras et al. (2019).

At the end of the day, with this method we can reproduce any neutrino cosmology, with neutrino mass \(M_\nu < 0.3 \, \mathrm{eV}\) with an accuracy of

  • \(1\%\) on all scales \(k < 1 \, h \, \mathrm{Mpc}^{-1}\) for the matter power spectrum
  • \(1\%\) for all mass bins with \(M < 10^{15} \, h^{-1} \, \mathrm{M}_\odot \) for the halo mass function
  • \(5\%\) for the subhalo (galaxy-like) redshift-space monopole and quadrupole of the 2-point correlation function

The build-up of strongly barred galaxies in the TNG100 simulation

go to paper

Bar structures are seen in more than one-third of disc galaxies observed today. We know now that they could play an important role in the evolution of the disc galaxies. Some studies (e.g. Gavazzi et. al. 2015, Spinoso et al., 2017) have pointed out that stellar bars could suppress star formation in the inner part of the galaxy and possible in the entire galaxy. Indeed, some differences are found in barred disc galaxies in comparison with disc galaxies without a bar. Barred galaxies host older populations of stars, with a smaller content of gas whereas unbarred disc galaxies are still forming stars and contain younger stars (e.g., Masters et al. 2012).

In this paper, we explore the evolution and buildup of the strongly barred galaxies using the cosmological hydrodynamical simulation TNG100. We find similar differences between strongly barred galaxies and unbarred galaxies at z=0: The majority of barred galaxies are quenched, whereas unbarred galaxies continue to be active on the main sequence of star-forming galaxies.

To understand these differences, we analyse the evolution of strongly barred disc galaxies at z=0 and compare them to the ones of unbarred disc galaxies. The figure below shows an example of the evolution of a disc galaxy with a strong bar at z=0. The surface density maps of the stellar, gas and star-forming gas components and summarise our findings The disc is formed early accompanied by episodes of high star formation rates for strongly barred galaxies. On the contrary, unbarred galaxies formed their disc later in time with a more gradual star formation history. We also find that supermassive black holes have already grown to their final masses when the bar is forming, suggesting a possible connection between the black hole and bar formation. To investigate in more detail about this connection, we are currently analysing the properties of barred galaxies in the TNG50 simulation, which is a highest resolution cosmological hydrodynamical simulation designed to look in detail the structures of the galaxies.
